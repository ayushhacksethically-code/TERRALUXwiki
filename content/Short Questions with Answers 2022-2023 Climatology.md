## Short Questions with Answers (3rd Semester Honours - 2022-2023): Climatology (CC5)

### 1) Potential Evapotranspiration (PET)

- Potential evapotranspiration (PET) is the total amount of evapotranspiration, or evaporation, that may occur if plenty of water is present in the soil, a water source, or plants.
- The actual amount of evapotranspiration is strictly limited by the available water source.
- PET data is utilized for water management in agriculture, building, and drainage studies.

### 2) Significance of the Ionosphere

- The ionosphere is the part of the Earth's atmosphere that is actively ionized by solar radiation.
- It plays an important role in atmospheric electricity and forms the inner edge of the magnetosphere.
- It has practical importance because it directly influences long-distance radio propagation to places across the Earth.

### 3) Relationship Between El-Niño and the Monsoon

- An El Niño event in the Pacific Ocean suppresses monsoon rainfall over India.
- El Niño refers specifically to an abnormal warming of surface waters in the equatorial Pacific Ocean.
- The opposite phase, La Niña, refers to the abnormal cooling of sea surface waters in the same equatorial Pacific region.
- La Niña is known to aid and enhance rainfall over India.

### 4) Aerosol

- An aerosol is a collection of solid particles or liquid droplets dispersed in the air.
- Common atmospheric examples include smoke, fog, sea spray, and pollution particles.
- Aerosol particles, such as dust, are essential for precipitation because they function as condensation and freezing nuclei.
- A suspension of small solid particles or liquid droplets in air or another gas is defined as an aerosol.

### 5) Solar Constant

- The solar constant can be defined as the total amount of solar energy received by a unit area in a unit of time.
- It is measured in Watts per meter square ($\text{W/m}^2$).
- The solar constant is measured on an area completely perpendicular to the incoming sunlight.
- It is used to determine the exact amount of energy received by a particular area per unit of time.

### 6) Distinguish Between cPks Air Mass and mTwu Air Mass

- In standard meteorological notation, air masses are represented by a combination of a lowercase letter indicating the thermal/moisture characteristic and an uppercase letter indicating the source latitude.
- **cPk Air Mass**:
  - `c`: Indicates a cold air mass.
  - `P`: Indicates a polar air mass.
  - `k`: Not a standard designation; it may be an error or a specific regional notation.
- **mTwu Air Mass**:
  - `m`: Indicates a maritime (moist) air mass.
  - `T`: Indicates a tropical air mass.
  - `w`: Not a standard designation; it may be an error or a specific regional notation.
  - `u`: Not a standard designation; it may be an error or a specific regional notation.

### 7) Inversion of Temperature

- Normally, atmospheric temperature decreases with an increase in elevation, a process called the *normal lapse rate*.
- At times, this environmental situation is reversed and the normal lapse rate is inverted.
- This reversed condition is termed an Inversion of temperature.

### 8) Thermal Equator

- The thermal equator is an imaginary line on the Earth's surface that connects points experiencing the highest mean temperatures.
- It differs from the geographic equator, which is based strictly on the Earth's axial rotation and divides the planet into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

### 9) Central Role of the Eye in Tropical Cyclones

- The eye is a central and critical feature in the development and structure of a tropical cyclone.
- A tropical cyclone (hurricane or typhoon) is a powerful, organized system of clouds and thunderstorms containing a low-pressure center.
- The eye is a localized region of relatively calm weather at the absolute center of the storm.
- The eye is completely encircled by the eyewall, which forms a ring of intense thunderstorms.
