## Process and forms of Condensation

### Process of Condensation

- Condensation is the process by which water vapour changes from a gaseous state to a liquid state.
- If air is cooled below its dew point, a portion of the water vapour changes to liquid; this does not take place automatically like in a sealed container.
- There are four important processes under which air cools below its dew point:
  1. Loss of heat by radiation.
  2. Contact with cold surfaces such as cool ground, leaves of plants, layers of snow, and icebergs.
  3. Mixing with cold air.
  4. Internal processes in adiabatic change involving no actual addition or withdrawal of heat (e.g., adiabatic cooling by expansion in a rising air current).

### Adiabatic Process of Cooling

- An adiabatic process is defined as one in which no heat is added to or subtracted from the air mass.
- Expansion cooling depends primarily on a parcel of air mass rising through the atmosphere to higher elevations.
- Air is a poor conductor of heat, so a vertically moving air mass retains its heat energy and keeps its thermal identity different from the surrounding air.
- When an air mass rises, it expands due to the decrease in pressure at higher elevations, increasing its volume.
- During expansion, the air mass does work against the surrounding air, consuming its internal heat energy and reducing its temperature.
- The rate of decrease of temperature of a moving air mass with height is called the adiabatic lapse rate (ALR).
- If the air mass remains dry, the rate of decrease is called the dry adiabatic lapse rate (DALR), which has a value of about $10^{\circ}C/\text{km}$.
- DALR is different from the normal lapse rate, which is also called the environmental lapse rate and is recorded by a thermometer carried by a rising balloon.
- The temperature of a rising air mass decreases until it becomes saturated, and further cooling results in condensation.
- During condensation, water vapours convert into liquid, releasing the latent heat of condensation into the moving air mass.
- As a result, the parcel of air mass cools more slowly than the dry adiabatic lapse rate.
- This lower cooling rate is called the saturated adiabatic lapse rate (SALR).
- Its value generally remains around $5^{\circ}C/\text{km}$, but it varies from about $4^{\circ}C/\text{km}$ for very humid air in equatorial regions to about $9^{\circ}C/\text{km}$ for cold air in polar regions.
- Condensation depends upon two variables: the amount of cooling and the relative humidity in the air.

### Forms of Condensation

- **1) Dew**: Dew occurs when moisture is deposited in the form of water droplets directly on cooler surfaces of solid objects (rather than nuclei in the air), such as stones, grass blades, and plant leaves. Ideal conditions include a clear sky, calm air, high relative humidity, and cold, long nights, requiring the dew point to be above freezing.
- **2) Frost**: Frost forms on cold surfaces when condensation takes place below the freezing point ($0^{\circ}C$), meaning the dew point is at or below freezing. Excess moisture is deposited as minute ice crystals instead of water droplets under the same ideal conditions as dew.
- **3) Fog and Mist**:
  - When the temperature of an air mass containing a large quantity of water vapour falls suddenly, condensation takes place within itself on fine dust particles, making fog a cloud with its base at or very near the ground. Fog and mist cause visibility to become poor or zero.
  - In urban and industrial centres, smoke provides plenty of nuclei that assist in the formation of fog and mist; this mixture of fog and smoke is described as smog.
  - The only difference between mist and fog is that mist contains more moisture, with each nucleus containing a thicker layer of moisture. Mists are frequent over mountains as rising warm air up slopes meets a cold surface. Fogs are drier than mist and are prevalent where warm air currents contact cold currents, acting as mini clouds where condensation occurs around dust, smoke, and salt particles.
- **4) Clouds**: A cloud is a mass of minute water droplets or tiny crystals of ice formed by the condensation of water vapour in free air at considerable elevations, taking various shapes based on height.
