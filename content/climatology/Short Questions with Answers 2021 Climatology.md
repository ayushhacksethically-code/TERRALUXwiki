### 1) Albedo

- Albedo is the amount of sunlight (solar radiation) reflected by a surface.
- It is expressed either as a percentage or as a decimal value, where 1 represents a perfect reflector and 0 signifies complete absorption of incoming light.
- In planetary science, the surface under discussion is almost always the surface of a planet like Earth.

### 2) Sunspots and Insolation

- Sunspots are temporary phenomena on the Sun's photosphere that appear visually as dark spots.
- They are caused by intense magnetic activity and are associated with variations in solar radiation.
- Sunspots do not have a direct and significant impact on Earth's insolation (the amount of solar radiation received at the Earth's surface).

### 3) Role of an 'Eye' in a Tropical Cyclone

- The eye is a central and critical feature in the structural development of a tropical cyclone.
- A tropical cyclone (also known as a hurricane or typhoon) is a powerful, organized system of clouds and thunderstorms with a low-pressure center.
- The eye itself is a region of relatively calm weather located at the exact center of the storm.
- The eye is completely surrounded by the eyewall, which is a ring of intense thunderstorms.

### 4) Mesopause

- The mesopause is the boundary layer between the Mesosphere and the Thermosphere located at approximately 90km above the surface.
- The altitude was historically considered to be around 85 km.
- Measurements at higher altitudes and modeling studies in the last decade reveal that the mesopause actually consists of two distinct minima: one at about 85 km and a stronger minimum at about 100 km.

### 5) Weather Associated with Mid-Latitude Cyclones

- Mid-latitude cyclones (extratropical cyclones or wave cyclones) are large-scale low-pressure systems that form in the middle latitudes, typically between 30 and 60 degrees North and South of the equator.
- These cyclones are associated with specific weather characteristics, and their life cycle explicitly involves development, a mature stage, and final dissipation.

### 6) Rossby Waves

- Rossby waves form when cold air from the polar region makes its way toward the Equator while warm air from the tropics moves toward the poles.
- They form because heat tends to move from low to high latitudes due to differences in received solar radiation.
- The existence of these waves explains the functioning of low-pressure cells (cyclones) and high-pressure cells that produce the weather of middle and higher latitudes.

### 7) Latent Heat of Condensation

- Latent heat is the thermal energy required to convert a substance from one phase to another.
- Condensation of water is the phase conversion process from a gaseous form to a liquid form.
- During condensation, heat is released to the surroundings because the water molecules come closer together and their kinetic energy decreases.
- Latent heat is calculated per unit mass and represents a constant quantity for a substance.

### 8) Air Mass Definition

- An air mass is a large body of air whose physical properties, especially temperature, moisture content, and lapse rate, are more or less uniform horizontally for hundreds of kilometers.
- According to A.N. Strahler and A.H. Strahler: *"a body of air in which the upward gradients of temperature and moisture are fairly uniform over a large area is known as an air mass."*
