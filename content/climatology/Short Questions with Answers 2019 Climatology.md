### 1) Insolation

- Only two billionths, or two units of energy out of 1,00,00,00,000 units of energy radiated by the sun, reaches the earth's surface.
- This small proportion of solar radiation is the only major source of energy on earth for most physical and biological phenomena.
- Incoming solar radiation received through short waves is formally termed 'Insolation'.

### 2) Hadley Cell

- The Hadley cell is one part of the tricellular global atmospheric circulation of air.
- It begins at the equator, where intense solar radiation warms the air and causes it to rise through convection.
- This rising convective air creates a low-pressure system at the surface.
- As the air rises, it cools and moves away from the equator, subsequently subsiding or sinking to cause high pressure in subtropical areas.

### 3) Ozone Hole

- Ozone holes refer to the depletion of the ozone layer present in the upper atmosphere (stratosphere) at the earth's polar regions.
- The ozone hole fails to stop solar radiation that is harmful to living things on earth.
- It causes harmful effects such as health problems, including skin cancer and eye damage.

### 4) Eye of the Cyclone

- The eye of a cyclone is a distinct and often circular region at the center of a tropical cyclone.
- It is a key architectural feature of mature and well-developed tropical cyclones, including hurricanes and typhoons.

### 5) Distinction Between Bw and Bs Climate

- The classifications "Bw" and "Bs" refer to specific dry climate subtypes within the Köppen climate classification system.
- The Köppen system categorizes global climates based on distinct temperature and precipitation patterns.
- The **Bw climate** is a subtype of dry climates (B) specifically associated with arid or desert climates featuring a warm or hot temperature regime.
- The **Bs climate** is a subtype of dry climates (B) associated with steppe or semi-arid climates.

### 6) Dry vs. Wet Adiabatic Lapse Rate

- The **dry adiabatic lapse rate** is the rate at which the temperature of a parcel of dry air changes as it rises or descends in the atmosphere without exchanging heat with its surroundings.
- The **wet adiabatic lapse rate** is the rate at which the temperature of a parcel of air changes as it rises or descends and becomes saturated.
- Saturation leads to the condensation of water vapor into clouds.

### 7) Geostrophic Wind

- Geostrophic wind is caused by the combination of two important atmospheric effects: the pressure gradient force and the Coriolis effect.
- The pressure gradient force occurs when air moves from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.
- The Coriolis effect acts as a diversion of the air in the Northern Hemisphere of the earth.
- The movement due to pressure combined with this directional diversion is called the geostrophic wind.

### 8) Relationship Between Thermal Gradient and Wind Movement

- A thermal (temperature) gradient creates a corresponding atmospheric pressure gradient.
- The pressure gradient force acts perpendicular to isobars (lines of constant pressure).
- Air moves along these pressure gradients from high-pressure areas to low-pressure areas.
- Because wind moves from areas of higher pressure to lower pressure, the direction of the wind is generally from colder regions to warmer regions.
- This movement is dynamically influenced by the specific distribution of high and low-pressure systems across the Earth's surface.

### 9) Occluded Front

- Occlusion means the blocking or sealing of existence; with respect to weather fronts, it signifies the removal of the fronts from the ground.
- Occlusion occurs when a cold air mass occupies the ground space surrounding hairpin-turned, clubbed warm and cold fronts.
- This type of front exists for only a short time, remaining suspended in the sky.
