### Introduction

- An air mass is defined as a large body of air whose physical properties, especially temperature, moisture, and lapse rate, are more or less uniform horizontally for hundreds of kilometers.
- The barotropic condition of the atmosphere is a theoretically ideal condition where surfaces of constant pressure are not intersected by isostereic (constant density) surfaces.
- In a vertical cross-section of a barotropic atmosphere, isobars (imaginary lines joining places of equal atmospheric pressure) and isotherms appear parallel, which means zero baroclinicity.
- The barotropic condition is a rare phenomenon and is found in the case of frontogenesis, separated by a baroclinic front zone.
- The baroclinic condition of the atmosphere is seen in comparatively narrow zones of thermal discontinuity, also known as fronts in air mass property, which are perhaps 1000 Km in width.
- Baroclinic zones are belts of sharp meteorological gradient recognized as distinct factors in the origin of mid-latitude disturbances.
- Instability in baroclinic conditions is associated with rising warm air which is driven by vertical wind shear and by the superimposition of tropospheric divergence over a baroclinic zone.
- The intensification of tropospheric divergence over a baroclinic zone further strengthens upper wind.
- Warm and cold air masses may be brought closer by induced circulation.
- Frontal weather largely depends on moisture and equilibrium properties of the rising air mass.
- A weak front has cumulus humilis cloud development.
- A strong front has cumulonimbus development.
