### Introduction

- Agriculture plays a vital role in the overall economy of India.
- According to the 2011 census, about 54.6% of the Indian population is directly or indirectly engaged in agriculture and allied activities.
- Climate is one of the most significant factors for deciding the cropping pattern in India.
- India broadly has a tropical monsoon climate.
- Regional variations exist in the climatic elements of India, which include rainfall, temperature, humidity, and wind velocity.
- Geographers give more importance to rainfall patterns than to temperature among these climatic elements.
- Blanford was the first geographer who attempted to demarcate the various climate zones in India.
- Dudley Stamp, Wladimir Koeppen, and Trewartha also attempted to classify the climate zones of India.

### Classification of Climate Zones According to Dudley Stamp

- Stamp used the $18^{\circ}C$ isotherm of monthly temperature for January to demarcate two primary climatic regions in India.
- The two primary regions are the Temperate or Continental zone in northern India and the Tropical zone in southern India.
- These two regions are further divided into eleven sub-regions depending upon the quantity of precipitation and temperature.

#### The Climatic Zones of Temperate or Continental India

- **1) The Himalayan Region (Heavy rainfall)**:
  Spreads over the states of Arunachal Pradesh, Sikkim, West Bengal, and large parts of Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, and the Union Territory of Jammu & Kashmir.
  During winter, the temperature of this region may reach around $4^{\circ}C$ to $7^{\circ}C$.
  The summer temperature is around $13^{\circ}C$ to $18^{\circ}C$.
  The eastern Himalayan region receives more rainfall, measuring around 200cm or more.
  The western Himalayan region receives less rainfall, measuring around 125cm.

- **2) The North-Western Region (Moderate Rainfall)**:
  Includes the northern parts of Punjab and some southern parts of the Union Territory of Jammu and Kashmir.
  The winter temperature of this region is around $16^{\circ}C$.
  The summer temperature is around $24^{\circ}C$.
  The region receives less rainfall than the Himalayan region, measuring around 40cm.

- **3) The Arid Low Land (or The Arid land)**:
  Includes the Indian desert (Thar desert), south-western parts of Haryana, and the Kutch region of Gujarat.
  The average temperature in winter is between $16^{\circ}C$ to $24^{\circ}C$.
  The region exhibits characteristics of continentality, causing the summer temperature to shoot up to $48^{\circ}C$.
  The average annual rainfall of this region is below 40cm.
  This region is marked by sand dunes.

- **4) The Region of Moderate Rainfall**:
  Includes parts of Punjab, Haryana, Western Uttar Pradesh, the National Capital Territory of Delhi (NCT), the north-western plateau of Madhya Pradesh, and the eastern parts of Rajasthan.
  Generally receives an average rainfall between 40cm to 80cm.
  The average winter temperature in January is between $15^{\circ}C$ to $18^{\circ}C$.
  The summer temperature in July may reach between $33^{\circ}C$ to $35^{\circ}C$.

- **5) The Transitional Zone**:
  Includes the eastern parts of Uttar Pradesh and Bihar.
  Geographically lies between the region of moderate rainfall in the west and the area of heavy rainfall in the east.
  The average annual rainfall of this region is between 100cm to 150cm.
  The average temperature during the summer in July is between $30^{\circ}C$ to $35^{\circ}C$.
  The average temperature during the winter in January is between $15^{\circ}C$ to $19^{\circ}C$.

#### The Climatic Zones of Tropical India

- **6) The Region of very Heavy Rainfall**:
  Includes the states of Meghalaya, Assam, Tripura, Mizoram, Nagaland, and Manipur.
  The average annual rainfall of this region is more than 200cm.
  During winters, the temperature in January remains around $18^{\circ}C$.
  The summer temperature in July remains between $32^{\circ}C$ to $35^{\circ}C$.
  Cherrapunji and Mawsynram in Meghalaya receive the maximum rainfall in this region.

- **7) The Region of Heavy Rainfall**:
  Includes the states of Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, West Bengal, Odisha, and parts of north-eastern Andhra Pradesh.
  The average annual rainfall of this region is about 100cm to 200cm.
  The rainfall trend in this region decreases from the eastern to the western areas.
  In January, the temperature ranges between $18^{\circ}C$ to $24^{\circ}C$.
  In July, the temperature ranges between $29^{\circ}C$ to $35^{\circ}C$.

- **8) The Region of Moderate Rainfall**:
  Receives rainfall between 50cm to 100cm.
  Geographically lies between the Western Ghats and the Eastern Ghats.
  Receives less rainfall compared to the Western Ghats because it is situated in a rain shadow region.
  During winters, the average temperature is about $18^{\circ}C$ to $24^{\circ}C$.
  The temperature during summer is more than $32^{\circ}C$.

- **9) The Konkan Coast**:
  Lies parallel to the south-west monsoon winds.
  Receives rainfall totaling more than 200cm.
  Enjoys the moderating effect of the Arabian Sea.
  The temperature of this region ranges between $24^{\circ}C$ to $27^{\circ}C$.

- **10) The Malabar Coast**:
  Extends from Goa in the north to Kanyakumari in the south.
  High rainfall over 250cm is caused by the Western Ghats acting as a barrier to the summer monsoon winds originating in the Arabian Sea, which strike perpendicular to the terrain.
  Enjoys the moderating effect of the Arabian Sea.

- **11) Tamil Nadu**:
  Includes the state of Tamil Nadu and adjoining areas of Andhra Pradesh.
  The average rainfall ranges between 100cm to 150cm.
  It remains devoid of south-west monsoon winds and receives negligible rainfall from them.
  It receives rainfall in November and December from the north-east monsoon winds (retreating monsoon).
  Generally, the temperature of this region ranges around $24^{\circ}C$.
