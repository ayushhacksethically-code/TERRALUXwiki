### Bergeron-Findeisen Process

- The Bergeron theory (Bergeron-Findeisen theory) was proposed around 1930 by T. Bergeron and subsequently developed by W. Findeisen.
- The theory provides a mechanism for the growth of raindrops in an ice/water cloud.
- It is based on the differential values for saturation vapour pressure over ice and supercooled-water surfaces.
- The nuclei that form water droplets far outnumber the nuclei that form ice crystals.
- At cloud temperatures above $-20^{\circ}C$ (the activation temperature for many deposition nuclei), supercooled water droplets are initially much more abundant than ice crystals.
- Single ice crystals may be surrounded by thousands to hundreds of thousands of supercooled water droplets.
- When ascending air currents rise, the temperature drops well below $-20^{\circ}C$, causing the supercooled droplets to freeze.
- A single ice crystal has the capability to change the whole cloud into an ice cloud because the saturation vapour pressure over ice is lower than over water at the same temperature.
- When air is saturated with respect to water, it is supersaturated with respect to ice.
- At a temperature of $-10^{\circ}C$, this vapour pressure translates into a relative humidity of 100 percent (saturation) for air surrounding water droplets and 110 percent (supersaturation) for air surrounding ice crystals.
- In response to supersaturated conditions, water vapour migrates towards ice crystals, and deposition starts on ice crystals (sublimation nuclei).
- The ice crystals grow and collect together to form snowflakes.
- Deposition removes water vapour from the clouds, thereby lowering the relative humidity.
- The relative humidity of the air surrounding water droplets drops below 100 percent, causing the droplets to vaporise.
- Snowflakes grow at the expense of supercooled water droplets under these conditions.
- Growing ice crystals may come into contact with supercooled liquid water droplets, which freeze onto or around the crystals immediately on contact, a growth process known as riming.
- Riming is a primary mechanism of hail formation.
- As crystals grow larger and heavier, they begin to fall.
- Falling ice crystals increase in number because small splinters become detached from large dendritic crystals and act as fresh nuclei, repeating the whole process.
- If air temperatures are below freezing point most of the way to the ground, crystals reach the earth's surface as snowflakes.
- If the air below the cloud is above freezing point, snowflakes melt and reach the ground as water droplets.
- The process may not always operate; rain in tropical regions is observed from clouds only about 2 km deep, where the temperature at the top of the cloud is above $0^{\circ}C$.
- In these warm tropical conditions, it is impossible for ice crystals to develop for the Bergeron-Findeisen process to operate.

#### Cloud Structural Zones

- **Ice Crystal (Bergeron) Process**: Noted as an "important precipitation process in the middle and high latitudes".
- **Altitude 7600 m (25,000 ft)**: Temperature is $-40^{\circ}C$; Zone consists of "Ice only (glaciated)"; Classified under "COLD CLOUDS".
- **Altitude 5500 m (18,000 ft)**: Temperature is $-20^{\circ}C$; Zone consists of "Mixed ice and water" with "more water droplets than ice crystals"; Classified under "COLD CLOUDS".
- **Freezing Level ($0^{\circ}C$)**: Boundary layer where cloud droplets are still composed of liquid water below the freezing level (supercooled); note states that pure water in a small amount (small cloud droplets) has a lower freezing temperature, and cold clouds may drop below $-40^{\circ}C$ before small droplets freeze into ice embryos that serve as condensation nuclei (ice nuclei).
- **Altitude 1000 m (3000 ft)**: Zone consists of "Liquid water only"; Classified under "WARM CLOUDS".

### Collision-Coalescence Process

- Collision-coalescence ideas were put forward by George Simpson and Mason.
- The process is based on the fact that clouds contain a variety of sizes of water droplets.
- Uniformly small droplets tend to move at the same speed in the cloud, but mixing them with larger, slow-moving droplets formed around hygroscopic nuclei encourages collisions and amalgamations.
- These ideas were modified by Langmuir, who pointed out that the terminal velocities of falling drops are directly related to their diameters.
- Droplet diameters are determined by the size of the condensation nuclei; drops that grow on large condensation nuclei become larger.
- Larger drops have a higher terminal velocity than smaller ones, causing them to collide with the smaller drops.
- Raindrops or ice crystals often stick after colliding and thus grow in finite steps (collide and coalesce).
- Larger drops fall faster as they overcome air resistance more easily, allowing them to catch up even more rapidly with other droplets and crystals; the larger they grow, the faster they grow.
- For collision to occur, several conditions are necessary.
  - Smaller droplets must be close to the axis of fall of the larger drop, otherwise they follow the air currents around the falling drop and no impact occurs.
  - As larger droplets descend, they produce a miniature air current (air stream) that blows small particles out of their path, sweeping the smaller drops away.
  - Colliding ice crystals bounce off each other when they are plane in shape, dry, and cold.
  - Colliding droplets may bounce away from each other because there is little surface tension.
  - Even with collision, growth will only occur if the two drops coalesce, which occurs most readily if the drops are of considerably different sizes and atmospheric electricity is present to hold them together.
  - If a droplet with a negative charge collides with a positively charged droplet, their electrical attraction binds them together.
  - Continued collision leads to coalescence, resulting in many large unstable drops which, on further disruption, produce several large drops; this cycle leads to many larger drops.

### Forms of Precipitation

- **1) Rain**:
  - Rain is any liquid that drops from the clouds in the sky, described specifically as water droplets of $0.5\text{ mm}$ or larger.
  - Droplets less than half a millimeter are defined as a drizzle.
  - Raindrops fall when small cloud particles strike and bind together to create bigger drops until they become too heavy to remain suspended, causing gravity to pull them down.
- **2) Snow**:
  - Snow occurs almost every time there is rain, though it often melts before reaching the earth's surface.
  - It is precipitation in the form of virga or flakes of ice water falling from clouds, normally seen together with high, thin, and weak cirrus clouds.
  - It can fall when atmospheric temperatures are above freezing, but it mostly occurs in sub-freezing air.
  - When temperatures are above freezing, snowflakes can partially melt, but evaporation occurs almost immediately due to relatively warm temperatures, leading to localized cooling that allows it to reach the ground as snow.
  - It has a fluffy, white, and soft structure formed in shapes like flat plates and thin needles under specific combinations of atmospheric humidity and temperature. The process is called snowfall.
- **3) Sleet (Ice Pellets)**:
  - Sleet takes place in freezing atmospheric conditions.
  - Also known as ice pellets, they form when snow falls into a warm layer, melts into rain, and the droplets then fall into a freezing layer of air cold enough to refreeze them.
  - It is defined as a form of precipitation composed of small, semitransparent balls of ice, which are smaller than hailstones.
- **4) Freezing Rain**:
  - Freezing rain happens when rain falls during below-freezing conditions, resulting in the solidification of rain droplets.
  - Raindrops are super-cooled while passing through a sub-freezing atmospheric layer and freeze by the time they reach the ground, coating cars, streets, trees, and power lines.
  - This even coating of ice is called glaze, which can build up to a thickness of several centimeters and poses a huge threat to roadway transportation, aircraft, and power lines.
- **5) Hail**:
  - Hailstones are big balls and irregular lumps of ice that fall from large thunderstorms, representing purely solid precipitation.
  - Unlike sleet, hailstones are predominantly experienced in winter or cold weather during thunderstorms.
  - They are mostly made up of water ice and measure between $0.2\text{ inches}$ ($5\text{ millimeters}$) and $6\text{ inches}$ ($15\text{ centimeters}$) in diameter, ranging from the size of a pea to larger than a grapefruit.
- **6) Drizzle**:
  - Drizzle is very light rain that is stronger than mist but less than a shower.
  - Drizzle droplets are smaller than $0.5\text{ millimeters}$ ($0.02\text{ inches}$) in diameter and arise from low stratocumulus clouds.
  - Due to their minute size, they sometimes evaporate before reaching the ground, but they can be persistent in cold atmospheric temperatures.
  - Mist is defined as a thin fog with condensation near the ground, while fog is made up of ice crystals or cloud water droplets suspended in the air near or at the earth's surface.
- **7) Sun Shower**:
  - A sun shower is a precipitation event registered when rain falls while the sun shines.
  - It occurs when winds bearing rainstorms blow them several miles away into an area without clouds, allowing sun's rays to penetrate the raindrops as a single rain cloud passes. It is most of the time accompanied by a rainbow.
- **8) Snow Grains**:
  - Snow grains are very small, white, and opaque grains of ice.
  - They are fairly flat with a diameter generally less than $1\text{ mm}$, making them almost equivalent to the size of a drizzle.
- **9) Diamond Dust**:
  - Diamond dust consists of extremely small ice crystals usually formed at low levels and at temperatures below $-30^{\circ}C$.
  - It is named for the sparkling effect created when light reflects on the suspended ice crystals.
- **10) Rime**:
  - When very small supercooled droplets strike subfreezing ground, they freeze immediately on impact and trap air between them.
  - It accumulates as a deposit of opaque, white, rough-textured ice crystals.
  - Rime ice builds up most rapidly in cool, humid conditions on surfaces exposed to the wind, sometimes accumulating as large cauliflower-shaped growths.
