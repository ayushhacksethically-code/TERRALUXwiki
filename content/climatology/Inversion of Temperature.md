## Inversion of Temperature

### Introduction

- Under normal environmental conditions, temperature usually decreases with an increase in altitude in the troposphere at a rate of 1 degree for every 165 meters.
- This standard decrease is formally called the normal lapse rate.
- On some occasions, situations get reversed and temperature starts increasing with height rather than decreasing, which is defined as temperature inversion.
- The environmental lapse rate varies from place to place and from time to time, particularly in the lowest few hundred meters of the troposphere.
- The average rate of temperature change within the troposphere is about $6.5^{\circ}C$ per 1000 meters ($3.6^{\circ}F$ per 1000 feet).
- This average rate is termed the average lapse rate or the average vertical temperature gradient.

### Favourable Conditions for Inversion of Temperature

- **1) Long Winter Nights**:
  - Terrestrial radiation is faster when the sky is clear during long winter nights because the earth is cooling off faster.
  - This rapid cooling cools the lower layer of the earth-bound atmosphere, while the upper layer remains warm.
- **2) Clear, Cloudless Sky**:
  - Clouds block the earth's terrestrial radiation, but this radiation passes unobstructed on cloudless days.
  - Unobstructed radiation causes the soil to cool faster, which consequently cools the air that comes into contact with it.
- **3) Dry Air**:
  - Moist air absorbs the earth's radiation, whereas dry air does not block radiation and allows it to escape into the atmosphere.
- **4) Ice-covered Area**:
  - Due to high albedo, less sunlight is received in ice-covered areas.
  - Most of the heat is lost into the atmosphere at night due to terrestrial radiation, cooling the surface and contact air while the upper layer remains warm.
- **5) Calm Atmosphere**:
  - The blowing of winds brings warm and cold air into contact, but under calm atmospheric conditions, cold air stays put near the ground.

### Types of Inversion of Temperature

- Temperature inversion is mainly classified into two types: Surface Inversion and Upper-air Inversion.

#### A. Surface Inversions

- Surface inversions are also called surface temperature inversions.
- This phenomenon occurs when air is cooled by coming in contact with a colder surface until it becomes cooler than the overlying atmosphere.
- It occurs mostly on clear nights when the ground cools rapidly by radiation.
- If the temperature of the surface air drops below its dew point, it may result in fog.
- The most visible inversions are those found at ground level.

##### 1) Radiational Inversion

- These develop on long, cold winter nights when a land surface rapidly emits longwave radiation into a clear, calm sky.
- Conduction cools the air immediately above as a result of the cold ground.
- In a relatively short time, the lowest few hundred meters of the troposphere become colder than the air above, putting a temperature inversion into effect.
- They primarily occur in winter because there is only a short daylight period for incoming solar heating and a long night for radiational cooling.
- They are much more prevalent in high latitudes than elsewhere.
- *Visual Diagram Labels:* night time, LW (longwave radiation), radiation inversion, warm air, cold ground, and heat transfer by conduction.

##### 2) Advection Inversions

- They develop when there is a horizontal inflow of cold air into an area.
- This setup is common when cool marine air blows into a coastal locale.
- Advection inversions are usually short-lived (typically overnight) and shallow.
- They can happen at any time of year, depending on the location of the relatively cold surface and the direction of the wind.
- *Visual Diagram Labels:* Warm air from land, Cool marine air, Prevailing winds, Ocean, and Land.

##### 3) Drainage Inversion

- This type of surface inversion results when cooler air slides down a slope into a valley, thereby displacing slightly warmer air.
- During winter, this fairly common occurrence in some mid-latitude regions is called a cold-air drainage inversion.
- *Visual Diagram Labels:* Cold air drains into the valley at night; Heat in the form of longwave radiation escapes into space at night.

#### B. Upper-Air Inversions

- Temperature inversions in the upper air are nearly always the result of air descending from above and are hence called subsidence inversions.
- Subsidence inversions are usually associated with high-pressure conditions.
- These conditions are particularly characteristic of subtropical latitudes throughout the year and of Northern Hemisphere continents in winter.
- A subsidence inversion can be fairly deep, sometimes spanning several thousand meters.
- Its base is usually a few hundred meters above the ground because low-level turbulence prevents the warmer air from sinking lower.
- *Visual Diagram Labels:* Under Air Above Inversion / Cooler Air Above Inversion, where air is heated as it is compressed into the side of the hill, resulting in Warm Air overriding Trapped Cooler Air.

### Consequences of Temperature Inversion

- **1) Formation of Fog & Low Visibility**:
  - Temperature inversions trap cooler, moisture-laden air near the ground, causing condensation that forms fog, especially on calm, clear nights.
  - Fog, haze, or smog resulting from inversions can drastically lower visibility, impacting transportation and everyday activities.
- **2) Atmospheric Stability & Suppressed Convection**:
  - Inversions act like a lid over the lower atmosphere, preventing warm air from rising.
  - This restricts convection and limits cloud formation, leaving clear skies above the inversion.
- **3) Surface Air Pollution & Smog**:
  - Dust, smoke, and industrial emissions often get trapped near the surface during inversions, deteriorating air quality and creating smog in urban and industrial zones.
- **4) Temperature Extremes & Diurnal Variation**:
  - The cool air under an inversion can cause chilly nights, while restricted mixing with upper layers reduces daytime temperature moderation.
- **5) Frost Damage to Agriculture**:
  - Radiation inversions can quickly lower ground temperatures, leading to frost that can harm crops and vegetation.
- **6) Stagnant Weather & Suppressed Precipitation**:
  - Inversions create stable atmospheric layers, slowing wind speeds and causing prolonged stagnant weather patterns in affected areas.
  - Vertical air movement is limited under inversions, suppressing rainfall and sometimes extending dry spells, contributing to drought conditions.
- **7) Severe Storm Potential**:
  - Strong inversions can prevent thunderstorms by blocking warm, moist air from rising.
  - If the inversion breaks, accumulated energy can trigger severe storms suddenly.
