## Temperate Cyclone

### Introduction

- A temperate cyclone is also referred to as a mid-latitude depression, extra-tropical cyclone, frontal depression, or wave cyclone.
- Temperate cyclones are active above the mid-latitudinal region between $35^{\circ}$ to $65^{\circ}$ latitude in both hemispheres.
- They move from west to east and are more pronounced during winter seasons.
- Polar and tropical air masses meet and form fronts within these latitudinal zones, and most cyclones form at a wavelike twist or perturbation on these fronts.
- On a weather map, they are shown as a low-pressure area enclosed by circular or elliptical isobars.
- When these enclosing isobars take an elongated shape, the pressure system is called a trough.
- These cyclones are mainly observed in the Atlantic Ocean and northwest Europe, with the characteristics to develop over both oceanic and land surfaces.
- They produce much of the highly variable and cloudy weather encountered in the temperate zone due to mid-latitude air mass convergence.

### Characteristics of Temperate Cyclone

- Extra-tropical cyclones are storm systems emerging in the mid and high latitudes, completely away from the tropics.
- They are low-pressure systems with associated cold fronts, warm fronts, and occluded fronts.
- They are also known as mid-latitude storms or baroclinic storms.
- In the Northern Hemisphere, cold air blows from the north of the front and warm air blows from the south.
- When pressure descents along the front, cold air moves southwards and warm air moves northwards, setting an anticlockwise cyclonic circulation in motion.
- This circulation results in a well-built extra-tropical cyclone with distinct warm and cold fronts, featuring pockets of warm air compressed between the forward and rear cold air.
- The warm air climbs over the cold air, causing a series of clouds to appear ahead of the warm front and produce rainfall.

### Front Types

- **Front**: Air masses develop over uniform regions with matching temperature, air pressure, and humidity. Based on temperature, they are warm or cold; when they march forward and converge, this convergence zone is known as a front.
- **Warm Front**: In the Northern Hemisphere, it develops to the southeast, to the right side of the moving direction of the warm air mass. In the Southern Hemisphere, it develops to the southeast, to the left side of the moving direction. The effect of the warm air mass is pronounced along it.
- **Cold Front**: In the Northern Hemisphere, it develops to the northwest, to the right side of the moving direction of the cold air mass. In the Southern Hemisphere, it develops to the southwest, to the left side of the moving direction. The effect of the cold air mass is marked along it.
- **Occluded Front**: Occlusion signifies the blocking, sealing, or removal of fronts from the ground. It occurs when a cold air mass occupies the ground space surrounding hairpin-turned, clubbed warm and cold fronts. These fronts exist for a short time suspended in the sky.

### Origin and Development of Temperate Cyclone (Polar Front Theory)

- Major advances were made towards the end of World War I by Norwegian meteorologists associated with the Bergen School of Meteorology, including Vilhelm Bjerknes, his son J. Bjerknes, and their associates.
- They identified the first model for the life cycle of mid-latitude cyclones, focusing on an occluded front where a cold front overtakes a warm front. Their work is called the Polar Front Theory or Wave Theory.
- According to this theory, a polar front forms a surface of discontinuity where warm-humid tropical air masses meet dry-cold polar air masses, occurring between sub-tropical highs and sub-polar lows.
- Cold air is denser and heavier because its intermolecular space is smaller, so it occupies the lower ground space and pushes warm sub-tropical air up.
- This interaction creates instability and a low-pressure area at the center of interaction where warm and cold fronts differentiate, which later develops into the eye of the cyclone.
- The formation of a wave indicates thermal contrast as the air masses encroach on each other's domains.
- In the Northern Hemisphere, the warm air mass moves northward as westerlies, and the cold air mass moves southward as easterlies.
- Winds bend to the right according to Ferrel's law; thus, the eastern part of the front is dominated by the warm air mass, while the western side is dominated by the cold air mass (reversed in the Southern Hemisphere).

#### The Five Successive Stages of Development

##### 1) Stage I: Stationary / Beginning

- Cold and warm air masses converge along an axis, remaining stable and in contact with each other in a stationary stage.
- Temperature, moisture, and pressure differences are marked but not large enough to create large-scale instability.
- Over time, the warm air mass pushes on the eastern side and the cold air mass pushes on the western side, turning the straight, flat front.

##### 2) Stage II: Beginning of Young Adult

- Frontiers become conspicuously different; the warm front lies to the eastern part and the cold front to the western part in the Northern Hemisphere.
- The area to the north/north-west occupied by cold air is the cold sector, and the area to the south/south-east dominated by warm air is the warm sector, dictated by the Coriolis Effect and Ferrel's law.
- The warm front has more instability due to higher moisture, while the cold front is relatively more stable.
- Instability is greatest at the junction of the two fronts; the cold air mass pushes the warm air mass, sharpening the junction, shrinking the warm sector, and sharpening the bend.
- Lighter warm air is pushed up over the cold air mass, cooling due to height and contact to cause condensation and precipitation.
- Released latent heat creates a further low pressure over the junction, initiating the cyclonic condition.

##### 3) Stage III: Mature

- Low air pressure at the junction induces a significant vertical updraft, attracting surrounding air.
- Intensification causes isobars to become closer and the pressure gradient to become very sharp.
- The updraft moves swiftly in a whirling manner, the cold air mass invasion increases, and the warm sector shrinks further with sharper frontal turns.
- Driven by prevailing westerlies, the cyclone marches eastwards, bringing changing weather conditions until the combined front lifts to signal diminishing strength.

##### 4) Stage IV: Occlusion

- Compression and diffusion cause the two fronts to come closer and merge into an occluded front.
- The cyclone declines as low pressure weakens, intensity lowers, and wind velocity decreases.
- The frontal convergence detaches from the ground and remains suspended in the sky; the ground is occupied entirely by cold air.
- The warm sector and atmospheric discontinuity are shifted entirely above the ground into the sky.

##### 5) Stage V: Late Occlusion / Dissipation

- The cyclone is substantially weakened by the end phase of occlusion.
- Dense, heavy cold air occupies more ground area and completely governs former warm air areas.
- Pushed-up warm air cools down completely via contact and adiabatic cooling.
- By the end of this stage, the low pressure and lifted occluded front are eliminated, returning the atmosphere to normal conditions.
