### Definition

- **Economic Geography** is a major branch of human geography that studies the spatial distribution of economic activities across the world.
- It examines how people produce, exchange, distribute, and consume goods and services in different geographic locations.
- It seeks to answer not only where economic activities occur but also why they are located there and how they are interconnected.

### Nature of Economic Geography

- **1) Dynamic Nature**: 
  - Economic geography is constantly changing due to technological progress, globalization, shifting trade patterns, and political policies. It is not static; spatial economic landscapes evolve over time.
- **2) Interdisciplinary Field**: 
  - It bridges geography with economics, sociology, political science, history, and environmental science to explain economic phenomena.
- **3) Spatial Emphasis**: 
  - Its core focus is spatial organization—understanding the geographic distribution of resources, industries, markets, and labor, and the connections between them.
- **4) Human-Environment Interaction**: 
  - It analyzes how humans utilize natural resources (such as minerals, forests, and water) and adapt their economic production to environmental constraints and opportunities.

### Scope of Economic Geography

The scope of economic geography covers all primary, secondary, tertiary, and higher-level economic activities:

- **1) Primary Activities**: 
  - Direct extraction of natural resources from the environment, such as agriculture, fishing, forestry, and mining.
- **2) Secondary Activities**: 
  - Manufacturing, industrial processing, construction, and value addition (e.g., converting iron ore into steel or processing fish for market sales).
- **3) Tertiary Activities**: 
  - Service industries connecting producers and consumers, including transportation, trade, banking, retail, and insurance.
- **4) Quaternary & Quinary Activities**: 
  - High-level decision making, research and development, information technology, and intellectual services.
- **5) Location Theories**: 
  - Formulating models to explain agricultural, industrial, and market locations (e.g., Von Thünen's model or Weber's location theory).
- **6) Resource Management & Sustainability**: 
  - Analyzing spatial resource allocation, economic distances, environmental impacts, and sustainable development strategies.
