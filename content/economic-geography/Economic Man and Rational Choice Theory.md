### 1) The Concept of Economic Man (Homo Economicus)

#### Definition

**Economic Man** (or *Homo Economicus*) is a theoretical concept in economics depicting a hypothetical human who makes completely logical, sensible, and rational decisions to maximize personal gain and utility.
- It serves as a foundational assumption for many classical and neoclassical economic models.
- The concept was popularized by Adam Smith, who explored how the pursuit of individual self-interest leads to efficient economic outcomes in his seminal book *The Wealth of Nations* (1776).

#### Key Characteristics

- **1) Perfect Rationality**:
  - Makes decisions objectively based on complete, accurate, and transparent market information.
- **2) Self-Interest Driven**:
  - Motivated purely by self-interest to enhance financial security, wealth, and personal satisfaction, often regardless of the cost to others.
- **3) Utility & Profit Maximization**:
  - Consistently aims to get the maximum value or satisfaction (utility) out of spent resources, and maximum profit as a producer.
- **4) Cost-Benefit Analysis**:
  - Carefully evaluates the financial costs versus benefits before choosing options offering maximum gain for minimal cost.
- **5) Absence of Emotion & Social Influence**:
  - Remains unaffected by social norms, peer pressure, empathy, revenge, or emotional factors.
- **6) Operates in Perfect Competition**:
  - Functions in markets with transparent pricing, zero barriers to entry/exit, and instant adjustment to changes.

#### Criticisms and Real-World Limitations

- **Bounded Rationality**: Herbert Simon proposed that humans have limited cognitive capacity, time, and access to information, meaning they "satisfice" (make decisions that are good enough) rather than maximize.
- **Psychological Biases**: Behavioral economics shows that humans are prone to emotional decisions, cognitive shortcuts, loss aversion, and peer influence.
- **Social Norms & Altruism**: Real humans often act out of charity, social duty, empathy, or moral values, which contradicts pure self-interest.

### 2) Rational Choice Theory

#### Definition

**Rational Choice Theory** posits that individuals use rational calculations to make choices that align with their personal goals and deliver the highest personal benefit or utility.

#### Core Assumptions

- **1) Risk vs. Reward**:
  - Assumes individuals act only when the anticipated reward equals or outweighs the associated risk. A choice with risk twice as great as reward is considered non-rational.
- **2) Utility vs. Price**:
  - In consumer behavior, individuals buy products where the total utility meets or exceeds the price ($\text{Utility} \ge \text{Price}$).
- **3) Resource Efficiency**:
  - Assumes decision-makers effectively utilize all accessible information and resources and put full effort into their decision-making.
- **4) Transitivity of Preferences**:
  - Assumes logical consistency in choices. If an individual prefers Option A over B, and Option B over C, they logically prefer Option A over C ($\text{A} > \text{B}$ and $\text{B} > \text{C} \implies \text{A} > \text{C}$).

#### Cross-Disciplinary Applications

- **Economics**:
  - Explains consumer purchasing patterns, pricing mechanisms, and supply-demand behavior.
- **Political Science**:
  - Models voter choices (voter turnout, party alignment), public choice theory, policy decisions, and international relations (e.g., predicting foreign leaders' actions and nuclear program choices to plan preemptive strategies).
- **Sociology**:
  - Explains social interactions as reciprocal cost-benefit exchanges. If a relationship or social interaction lacks perceived benefit or mutual exchange, a person will choose not to engage. It is also applied in social work to understand the decision-making of low-income families in constrained environments.
- **Addiction Studies**:
  - Explains early addiction pathways as risk-benefit calculations, where the short-term benefit of alleviating negative feelings or achieving euphoria temporarily outweighs the long-term risk of dependency.
