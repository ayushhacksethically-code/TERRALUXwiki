## Von Thünen's Agricultural Model

### 1) Introduction & Background

- **Johann Heinrich von Thünen** (1783–1850) was a pioneering German economist and estate owner of the early 19th century.
- In **1826**, he formulated his agricultural location theory based on a detailed econometric analysis of his estate, **Tellow**, in Mecklenburg near Rostock in north-eastern Germany.
- Most of the empirical data used to explain his theory was obtained through his practical experience managing this estate.
- **Main Aim**: Von Thünen attempted to construct a theoretical model of land-use patterns to show *how* and *why* agricultural land-use types and intensities vary with distance from a central market.
- **Core Premise**: If environmental variables (such as soil fertility and climate) are held constant, the farm product that achieves the highest profit will outbid all other products in the competition for location.
- **Locational Rent (Economic Rent)**: The bidding power of a crop or livestock activity depends on the level of anticipated financial return (locational rent) from producing at a specific location. Products with high expected returns have high rent-paying ability and can outbid products with lower profit levels.

---

### 2) Assumptions of Von Thünen's Theory

To construct his model, von Thünen made the following simplifying assumptions about an "Isolated State":

- **1) The Isolated State**:
  - The state is completely cut off from the rest of the world and consists of exactly **one central market city** and its agricultural hinterland.
- **2) Exclusive Market**:
  - The central city is the sole market for surplus products from the hinterland and receives products from no other external areas.
- **3) Exclusive Supply**:
  - The hinterland ships its agricultural surpluses to no other market except the central city.
- **4) Homogeneous Physical Environment**:
  - A uniform plain surrounds the city. The climate, soil fertility, and topography are identical everywhere, meaning there are no natural advantages for any particular crop.
- **5) Rational, Profit-Maximizing Farmers**:
  - The hinterland is inhabited by rational farmers who wish to maximize profits and adjust their crop choices automatically to market demand and prices.
- **6) Single Mode of Transport**:
  - Transportation is restricted to a single mode—**horse and wagon** (reflecting the technology of 1826). There are no railways or paved highways.
- **7) Proportional Transport Costs**:
  - Transportation costs are directly proportional to distance and weight, borne entirely by the farmers. All produce is shipped in a fresh state.

### 3) Concentric Land-Use Zones

Based on transport costs and crop yields, von Thünen calculated the locational rent for different activities at various distances from the market. This resulted in a pattern of **six concentric rings** around the market city:

```
 [ Zone VI: Livestock Farming / Ranching ]
   [ Zone V: Three-Field System ]
     [ Zone IV: Crop Farming, Fallow & Pasture ]
       [ Zone III: Crop Farming without Fallow (Rye) ]
         [ Zone II: Firewood & Lumber Production ]
           [ Zone I: Market Gardening & Milk ]
                 [ Central City Market ]
```

- **Zone I: Market Gardening & Milk Production**
  - **Location**: Located closest to the city center.
  - **Rationale**: Fresh milk, fruits, and vegetables are highly perishable and must reach the market quickly. Their production is intensive and utilizes manure brought back from the city (short distance transport of fertilizer).
- **Zone II: Firewood & Lumber Production (Forestry)**
  - **Location**: The second ring.
  - **Rationale**: Wood was in high demand in the 19th century as a source of fuel and building material. Because timber is heavy and bulky, transport costs are extremely high. Locating forestry close to the city minimizes these shipping expenses.
- **Zone III: Crop Farming without Fallow**
  - **Location**: The third ring.
  - **Rationale**: Intensive crop farming where **rye** is the primary market product. No fallowing is practiced, and fertility is maintained through heavy cropping rotation. The intensity of rye production and crop yields decrease as distance from the market increases.
- **Zone IV: Crop Farming, Fallow & Pasture**
  - **Location**: The fourth ring.
  - **Rationale**: Farming is less intensive, utilizing a 7-year crop rotation system: 1 year of rye, 1 year of barley, 1 year of oats, 3 years of pasture, and 1 year of fallow. Marketed products include rye, butter, cheese, and occasionally live animals for slaughter. These products do not perish quickly, allowing production at greater distances.
- **Zone V: Three-Field System**
  - **Location**: The fifth ring.
  - **Rationale**: Follows a traditional three-field crop rotation: 1/3 field crops (rye/grain), 1/3 pasture, and 1/3 left fallow. It supplies grain (rye) to the city at low intensity.
- **Zone VI: Livestock Farming / Ranching**
  - **Location**: The outermost ring.
  - **Rationale**: Dedicated entirely to livestock ranching. Rye produced here is solely for local farm consumption because transport costs prevent grain from generating profit. Only animal products (butter, cheese) and live animals (which can walk to the market themselves, minimizing transport charges) are shipped to the city.

---

### 4) Modified Conditions (Real-World Adjustments)

Von Thünen recognized that the real world does not feature a perfectly uniform plain. He modified his model to illustrate how spatial factors alter the concentric rings:

- **Navigable River**:
  - The introduction of a navigable river extending outward from the central city. Since water transport by barge is much cheaper than transport by horse and wagon, the cost contour lines stretch along the river, converting the circular rings into elongated, linear bands along the river banks.
- **Secondary Sub-Center**:
  - The introduction of a smaller secondary market town (sub-center) in the hinterland. This sub-center develops its own smaller, localized concentric belts, interrupting the primary concentric zones of the central city.

---

### 5) Modern Relevance & Applications

Despite being formulated in 1826, the core concepts of Von Thünen's model remain highly relevant:

- **1) Bid-Rent Theory**:
  - The model serves as the direct foundation for modern urban land-use theories (e.g., William Alonso's Bid-Rent Theory and the Burgess Concentric Zone Model), explaining how land values decrease outward from the Central Business District (CBD).
- **2) Peri-Urban Agriculture**:
  - It explains why high-value, highly perishable crops (such as flowers, fresh berries, and organic dairy products) are grown in greenbelts immediately surrounding modern metropolitan areas.
- **3) Transport Economics**:
  - It highlights how transport cost variations dictate location decisions for commercial enterprises and supply chains in globalized markets.
- **4) Developing Economies**:
  - The model continues to reflect agricultural land-use zones in developing countries that rely on primitive transport infrastructure (like dirt roads and animal-driven carts) connecting rural villages to central markets.
