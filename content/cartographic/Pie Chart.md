## Pie Chart / Proportional Circles

### Definitions

- A pie chart, also known alternately as a circle graph or pie diagram, is a type of graph that uses a circular structure to view data.
- The pieces of the graph are equal to the percentage of the total in each group, making the size of each slice proportional to the size of the group as a whole.
- The entire pie represents 100% of the total, while the individual slices represent parts of that whole.

### Construction Methodology

1. **Data Collection and Categorization:** Gather categorical or nominal data that can be divided into distinct categories.
2. **Calculate Total:** Sum all values in the data to find the total, which represents the entire circle ($100\%$).
3. **Calculate Percentages:** Divide each category's individual value by the total and multiply by 100.
4. **Determine Angle Measures:** Convert the percentages into angle measures; since a full circle has 360 degrees, multiply the proportion to find the degree share of the circle.
5. **Draw a Circle:** Use a compass or drawing tool to create a perfect circle.
6. **Divide the Circle:** Section the circle into slices using the calculated angle measures, keeping sizes proportional to their percentages.
7. **Label the Slices:** Label each slice with its category name and corresponding percentage or value for interpretation.
8. **Coloring the Slices:** Apply unique colors or patterns to each slice to make them visually distinguishable.
9. **Add a Legend:** Include a legend near the chart to explain the color/pattern categories.

### Evaluative Data Points

#### Advantages
- Provides a straightforward, simple, and easy-to-understand visual illustration.
- Portrays data as a fraction of a whole, serving as an effective communication tool for inexperienced audiences.
- Allows viewers to perform immediate analysis by seeing data comparisons at a single glance.
- Eliminates the need for readers to examine or quantify raw underlying numbers.
- Data can be manipulated within the chart to highlight specific points the creator wants to make.
- Visually pleasing layout, making it great for gaining viewer attention.

#### Disadvantages
- Loses its effectiveness when there are too many data points.
- A high number of data pieces becomes confusing and difficult to read, even when adding labels and numbers.
- Reflects only one data set at a time, requiring a series of charts to compare different settings or time periods.
- Can make it challenging for readers to assimilate knowledge efficiently.
- Comparing non-adjacent slices or exact angles can be physically difficult for the reader's eye.
- It is not a good choice when the dataset contains negative numbers.

### Proportional Circle vs. Pie Diagram

#### Proportional Circle
- **Visual Representation:** Used in cartography as a type of thematic map where each circle's absolute size is directly proportional to the value of the data attribute represented (larger circles equal larger values, smaller equals smaller).
- **Data Representation:** Used to represent numerical data associated with specific geographic locations, showing spatial patterns and variations across regions on a map.
- **Best Suited For:** Visualizing quantitative data linked to specific places, such as regional population distributions or sales revenue by region.

#### Pie Graph / Pie Chart
- **Visual Representation:** Uses sectors or slices of a circle to represent different parts of a whole, where the entire circle represents 100%.
- **Data Representation:** Illustrates the composition or distribution of different parts within a whole, showing how individual components contribute and allowing comparison of relative category sizes.
- **Best Suited For:** Representing categorical or nominal data where parts add up to a whole, such as survey results, market shares, and budget allocations.
