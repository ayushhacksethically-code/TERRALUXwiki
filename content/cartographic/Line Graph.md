## Line Graphs

### General Definition

- A line graph is a unique graph commonly used in statistics that represents the change in a quantity with respect to another quantity.
- Variation in a line graph is usually plotted in a two-dimensional XY plane.
- If the relation including any two measures can be expressed utilizing a straight line in a graph, then such graphs are called linear graphs.
- While a linear graph must be a straight line, a line graph can consist of straight segments or curves representing non-linear variations over time or other intervals.
- A line graph, line chart, or line plot is a graph that utilizes points and lines to represent change over time.
- It is a chart that shows a line joining several points or a line that shows the relation between points.
- The graph represents quantitative data between two changing variables with a line or curve that joins a series of successive data points.
- Linear graphs compare two variables in a vertical axis and a horizontal axis.

### Types of Line Graphs

- **Simple Line Graph:** Plotted with only a single line to show the relationship between two variables. Usually, one variable is independent (typically time or other factors) and plotted on the horizontal axis, and the other is a dependent variable.
- **Multiple Line Graph:** Plotted with two or more lines to depict two or more variables that change over the same period of time. The independent variable is on the horizontal axis, and the two or more dependent variables are on the vertical axis (e.g., comparing full-time and part-time student populations over a couple of years).
- **Compound Line Graph:** Used when information can be subdivided into different types. It shows the total data proportion and the different layers making up the data. Multiple lines are constructed first, and each part is shaded to indicate its component contribution to the total. Each bottom line indicates a part of the total, the top line represents the absolute total, and the distance between any two consecutive lines shows the size of that specific part, with the bottom line bounded by the origin.

### Merits and Demerits

- **Advantages:**
  - Useful for representing continuous data, such as change over time.
  - Allows possible extrapolation of data.
  - Having a line constructed from multiple data points allows estimates of missing data.
  - Allows comparison of two or more items to see if there is any kind of connection or relationship.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Can be difficult to determine exact values at a given point on the graph.
  - Too many lines, or two lines with values that are too similar, can make comparing data difficult.
