## Coordinate Systems

### General Concepts

- A coordinate system is a method for identifying the location of a point on the earth.
- Most coordinate systems use two numbers, collectively called a coordinate, to identify a point's location.
- Each of these numbers indicates the distance between the point and some fixed reference point called the origin.
- The first number, known as the X value, indicates how far left or right the point is from the origin.
- The second number, known as the Y value, indicates how far above or below the point is from the origin.
- The origin point possesses a fixed coordinate of 0, 0.

### Number Line System

- The simplest example of a coordinate system is the identification of points on a line with real numbers using the concept of the number line.
- In a number line system, an arbitrary point O (the origin) is chosen on a given line.
- The coordinate of a point P is defined as the signed distance from point O to point P.
- The signed distance is taken as positive (+ve) or negative (-ve) depending on which side of the line point P lies.
- Each point on the number line is assigned a unique coordinate, and each real number is the coordinate of a unique point on the line.

### Common Types of Coordinate Systems

- There are three types of coordinate systems commonly used:
  1. Cartesian coordinate system
  2. Polar coordinate system
  3. Spherical coordinate system

#### 1) Cartesian Coordinate System
- A Cartesian coordinate system is a two-dimensional coordinate system that uses Cartesian coordinates to specify the position of points on a plane.
- It uses two perpendicular lines, called axes, to uniquely identify every point.
- The x-axis is the horizontal line that goes through the system, and the y-axis is the vertical line that goes through the system.
- A point's position is specified by its distance from the origin along each axis, where the origin is the exact point where the axes intersect.
- The system is a convenient way to specify points in a plane, or within a three-dimensional space when extended to three dimensions.
- Limitation: Many real-world problems are best described in terms of points in a curved space rather than a flat plane.
- Example: The path of a projectile is best described in terms of points in a three-dimensional space, even though the path itself is actually a two-dimensional curve in a plane.

#### 2) Polar Coordinate System
- The polar coordinate system is a coordinate system that uses two angular coordinates to uniquely identify a point in two-dimensional space.
- The radial coordinate is the distance from the origin to the target point.
- The angular coordinate is the angle, measured in radians, between the positive x-axis and the line segment connecting the origin to the point.
- A polar coordinate is specified by its radial coordinate $r$ and its angular coordinate $\theta$.
- The point $(r, \theta)$ lies on the unit circle when it has its center at the origin and a radius of 1.

#### 3) Spherical Coordinate System
- In mathematics, the spherical coordinate system is a coordinate system for three-dimensional space using three distinct coordinates: latitude, longitude, and altitude (or depth).
- It serves as an extension of the Cartesian coordinate system, where the three perpendicular axes are replaced by three coordinates defining a point in a curved space.
- Latitude (abbreviation: lat., $\phi$) is the angular distance from the equator, measured in degrees north or south of the equator.
- Longitude (abbreviation: long., $\lambda$) is the angular distance from the prime meridian, measured in degrees east or west of the prime meridian.
- Altitude (abbreviation: alt., $h$) or depth (abbreviation: dep., $z$) is the distance from the surface of the Earth to the specified point.

### Differences Between Cartesian and Polar Coordinates

- Cartesian coordinates use number lines as axes, and can be used in one, two, or three dimensions, giving them the ability to represent linear, planar, and solid geometries.
- Polar coordinates use an angle and a length as coordinates, and can directly represent only linear and planar geometries.
- Polar coordinates can be further developed into a cylindrical coordinate system to represent solid geometries.
- Both systems can represent imaginary numbers by defining an imaginary axis, playing a vital role in complex algebra.
- In plain form, Cartesian coordinates are real numbers $(x, y, z)$.
- Coordinates in the polar system are not always real numbers: if the angle is given in degrees, the coordinates are not real numbers; if the angle is given in radians, the coordinates are real numbers.
