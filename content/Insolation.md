## Insolation

### Introduction & Definition

- The radiant energy received from the sun, transmitted in a form analogous to shortwave (1/250 - 1/6700 mm in length), and travelling at the rate of 186000 miles a second is called solar radiation or Insolation.
- Insolation is measured by the amount of solar energy received per square centimetre per minute.
- The sun is the primary source of energy on the earth.
- This energy is radiated in all directions into space through short waves, which is known as solar radiation.
- Only two billionths, or two units of energy out of 1,0,00,00,000 units of energy radiated by the sun, reaches the earth's surface.
- This small proportion of solar radiation is the only major source of energy on the earth for most physical and biological phenomena.
- Incoming solar radiation through short waves is termed 'Insolation'.
- The amount of insolation received on the earth's surface is far less than that radiated from the sun because of the small size of the earth and its distance from the sun.
- Water vapour, dust particles, ozone, and other gases present in the atmosphere absorb a small amount of insolation.

### Factors Affecting Insolation

- The amount of insolation received on the earth's surface is not uniform everywhere and varies according to place and time.
- Tropical regions receive maximum annual insolation, which gradually decreases towards the poles.
- Insolation happens more in summers and less in winters.

#### 1) The Angle of Sun's Rays

- Since the earth is a geoid resembling a sphere, the sun's rays strike the surface at different angles at different places based on latitude.
- The higher the latitude, the less is the angle the sun's rays make with the surface of the earth.
- The area covered by vertical rays is always less than that covered by slant rays.
- If more area is covered, the energy gets distributed, and the net energy received per unit area decreases.
- Sun's rays with a small angle traverse more of the atmosphere than rays striking at a large angle.
- Visual labels from the text indicate:
  - **Ray a**: Corresponds to long distance travel through the atmosphere and covers a large area on the Earth.
  - **Ray b**: Corresponds to short distance travel through the atmosphere and covers a small area at the equator.

#### 2) Duration of the Day

- The length of the day determines the duration of sunlight, affecting the total amount of solar radiation received by the earth's surface.
- A longer period of sunshine allows a greater quantity of solar radiation to be received by a portion of the earth.
- At the equator, the length of days and nights is 12 hours in all months.
- At the tropics of the Arctic and Antarctic, sunshine duration varies between 0 and 24 hours.
- On the autumnal and spring equinoxes (September 23 and March 21 respectively), the sun is overhead at the equator at noon.
- On equinox days, night and day all over the earth are equal, and the maximum amount of insolation is received at the equator.
- Insolation decreases from the equator towards the poles because of vertical sunshine at the equator and increasingly slanting rays at higher latitudes.
- Structural earth orientations mentioned include:
  - September 22-23: Autumnal Equinox
  - March 20-21: Vernal Equinox
  - June 20-22: Summer Solstice
  - December 21-22: Winter Solstice (with an axial tilt marker of $23.5^{\circ}$)

#### 3) Earth's Distance from Sun

- The earth revolves around the sun in an elliptical orbit, resulting in a continuous annual change in the distance between the sun and the earth.
- This elliptical track leads to seasonal variations in the solar energy received by the earth.
- The mean distance between the earth and sun is about 149,600,000 kilometers (92,900,000 miles).
- **Aphelion**: Occurs on July 4 when the earth's position is farthest from the sun at a distance of 152 million km (152.1 million km / 94.5 million mi).
- **Perihelion**: Occurs on January 3 each year when the earth reaches its closest distance to the sun at 147 million km (147.1 million km / 91.4 million mi).
- During aphelion, the northern hemisphere faces the sun and receives about 7 percent less energy than the southern hemisphere experiences during perihelion.

#### 4) Sunspots

- Sunspots are defined as dark, cooler areas visible on the solar surface within the photosphere of the sun and surrounded by the chromosphere.
- These dark areas are cool because they are characterized by $1500^{\circ}C$ less temperature than the chromosomes/chromospheres that surround them.
- The energy radiated from the sun increases when the number of sunspots increases, consequently increasing the amount of insolation received at the earth's surface.
- Recent research shows that more and more energy is released when sunspots are in large numbers.
- The number of sunspots increases or decreases on a regular basis, creating a cycle of 11 years.

#### 5) Transparency of the Atmosphere

- The atmosphere is not transparent for all incoming radiation from the sun due to its differing composition and distinct layers.
- Atmospheric transparency is one of the controlling factors determining how much insolation reaches the earth's surface.
- The atmosphere is composed of gases, water vapour, and particulate matter.
- The atmosphere is a mixture of specific gases, including:
  - Nitrogen ($\text{N}$)
  - Oxygen ($\text{O}_2$)
  - Argon
  - Carbon dioxide
  - Neon ($\text{Ne}$)
  - Helium ($\text{He}$)
  - Methane ($\text{CH}_4$)
  - Krypton ($\text{Kr}$)
  - Ozone ($\text{O}_3$)
  - Nitrous oxide ($\text{N}_2\text{O}$)
  - Hydrogen ($\text{H}$)
  - Xenon ($\text{Xe}$)
- The atmosphere also contains water vapour, which represents water in its gaseous state.
